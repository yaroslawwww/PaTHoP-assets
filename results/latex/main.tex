\newpage\section*{Abstract}
This work is dedicated to investigating the improvement of forecasting accuracy for
chaotic time series by augmenting the training sample with data
from similar dynamical systems. The primary method used is an
algorithm based on finding characteristic sequences (motifs),
which is effective for multi-step ahead forecasting but is
demanding in terms of data volume. The possibility of compensating for the
lack of data in the main series by adding time series,
generated by the Lorenz system with close control parameters, is investigated.
Experiments were conducted demonstrating that this approach allows for
a significant reduction in the number of non-predictable points, while
  maintaining an acceptable level of forecast accuracy.
  \subsection*{Keywords}
Chaotic time series forecasting, Lorenz system,
training sample augmentation, motif method, non-predictable points

\section{Introduction}
The forecasting of chaotic time series is fundamentally limited by a
 prediction horizon, beyond which sensitivity
  to initial conditions leads to an exponential growth of error. \cite{MalinetskiyPotapov2002, Gilmore1984}
  Traditional iterative methods, which predict one step ahead,
   rapidly accumulate this error, making them ineffective for long-term
    forecasts. An alternative is provided by methods based on finding characteristic
    sequences (motifs), which reconstruct the system's dynamics in
    phase space and search for analogues of the current state in the past.

However, the practical effectiveness of such methods runs into the curse of
dimensionality. For a reliable reconstruction of the attractor and finding a statistically
 significant number of motifs, a data volume $N$ is required that grows exponentially
  with the embedding dimension $d$. Even in an idealized situation where the attractor
   is a $d$-dimensional cube with side $a$, its adequate filling
    requires $N \sim \frac{a^d}{(0.1a)^d} = 10^d$ points. For real systems,
    whose dynamics unfold on fractal attractors, the situation is exacerbated:
     $N > N_0 \cdot 10^d$, where the multiplier $N_0 \sim \epsilon_{fr}^{-d}$ depends
     on the scale of fractality $\epsilon_{fr}$. In practice, for example, for financial
      data, this may require on the order of $10^9$ observations.

This problem is aggravated by the requirement of data stationarity. The dynamical system
generating the time series may change its properties over time, which
makes old segments of the series irrelevant for describing the current dynamics. Thus,
an irresolvable contradiction arises: to overcome the curse of dimensionality,
 an enormous volume of data is required, but the stationarity requirement strictly limits
 the length of the sample that can be considered homogeneous and suitable for training.

The approach proposed in this work is an attempt to resolve this contradiction.
 We pose the key research question: \textbf{is it possible to compensate for the
  data deficit in the target time series by incorporating samples from other,
   formally independent, but behaviorally similar processes?} Our central hypothesis
    is that different, but related, processes may share a common
     vocabulary of local behavioral patterns (motifs). In this case, one could
     combine several shorter, but stationary, samples from different sources
      to form a single one, rich enough for training. The statistical benefit
      from creating such a diverse library of motifs may outweigh the noise introduced
      by data from a non-identical source.

To rigorously test this hypothesis, the Lorenz system was chosen
 as a controlled environment, allowing the degree of dissimilarity between series to be precisely dosed.
  Within the scope of this research, the following tasks are set:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item To quantitatively assess the impact of sample augmentation on forecast accuracy
    (RMSE, MAPE) and the proportion of non-predictable points.
    \item To determine the applicability limits of the approach by investigating the dependence
     of forecast quality on the magnitude of the parameter divergence between the target and auxiliary systems.
    \item To test the possibility of constructing a forecast for the target series
    based exclusively on data from auxiliary systems.
\end{enumerate}
Thus, this work aims to investigate the practical feasibility and limitations of the inter-series data augmentation method as a tool to combat information scarcity in the forecasting of chaotic processes.

\section{Literature Review}

The basis for the present study is the work of V.A. Gromov and F.S. Baranov \cite{GromovBaranov2023},
in which a method for forecasting chaotic time series is proposed,
 based on the search for characteristic sequences (motifs).
  The essence of the approach lies in the reconstruction of the phase space of the
   system from the observed time series and the subsequent clustering of
   state vectors to identify repeating patterns of dynamics.

A key feature of the method is the use of a multitude of motifs,
which generates a set of possible forecast values for each point.
 To obtain a single forecast (unification), previous works
  have considered various approaches, including calculating the mean
   value, applying weighted averaging (depending on the forecast length or the distance
    to the cluster center), and selecting the center of the largest cluster from the possible forecasts.

A crucial aspect that distinguishes this approach is the concept of
non-predictable points. It allows the algorithm to assess its own confidence
 and to refrain from forecasting in situations of high uncertainty,
 which increases the model's reliability. To identify such points, both
 statistical criteria (entropy of the set of forecast values, its
 multimodality, the width of the interquartile range) and more complex
 classifiers based on machine learning methods have been applied, including logistic regression,
  support vector machines (SVM), decision trees, k-NN, multilayer perceptrons (MLP), and their ensembles.\cite{Wang2019}

Studies have shown the high effectiveness of the method: the algorithm demonstrated
a nearly constant forecast error (MAE, RMSE) even as the
horizon was increased to 100 steps ahead, which was achieved through an exponential
 growth in the number of points classified as non-predictable. Of particular importance
  for the present work is the study \cite{Gromov2021}, which investigated the influence
  of the training sample size on accuracy. It was established that as the volume of
  data from the *original* series increased, the forecast error decreased logarithmically. However,
  the question of whether the lack of data could be compensated for by adding
  samples from *other, but dynamically similar* systems remained open.
 This work is aimed at investigating this very possibility.
\subsection{Formal Problem Statement}

This work is dedicated to the problem of multi-step forecasting of chaotic
 time series under data scarcity conditions. We consider a situation
  where a set of time series $\mathcal{Y} = \{Y^{(0)}, Y^{(1)},
   \dots, Y^{(M)}\}$ is available, where $Y^{(0)}$ is the \textit{target} series for which
    a forecast needs to be constructed, and $Y^{(1)}, \dots, Y^{(M)}$ are \textit{auxiliary} series.
     It is assumed that the auxiliary series are generated by dynamical systems
      that are similar, but not necessarily identical, to the target one.

All time series $Y^{(m)} = \{y_0^{(m)}, y_1^{(m)}, \dots\} \in \mathcal{Y}$ satisfy the following assumptions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item In each series, all transient processes have completed, and the observed
    dynamics occur in the vicinity of a strange attractor.
    \item Each series satisfies the conditions of Takens' theorem\cite{Takens1981}, which makes
    a correct reconstruction of the attractor from one-dimensional observations possible.
\end{enumerate}

To formulate the forecasting problem and its subsequent evaluation, the data is divided
 as follows. The target series $Y^{(0)}$ is split into two non-overlapping parts:
  a training (observed) sample $Y_1^{(0)} = \{y_0^{(0)}, \dots, y_t^{(0)}\}$ and
   a test sample $Y_2^{(0)} = \{y_{t+1}^{(0)}, y_{t+2}^{(0)}, \dots\}$, on which
    the forecast quality is evaluated. The auxiliary series $Y^{(1)}, \dots, Y^{(M)}$
     are used for training in their entirety. Thus, the complete training set
     $\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}$ is defined as the union of the observed part of
      the target series and all auxiliary series:
\[
\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}} = \{Y_1^{(0)}, Y^{(1)}, \dots, Y^{(M)}\}.
\]

A key feature of the proposed approach is that for each
 point to be predicted, $y_{t+h}^{(0)}$, from the test sample $Y_2^{(0)}$, the algorithm
  generates not a single value, but a \textit{set of possible predictions}
   $\hat{S}_{t+h}$. This set is formed by a function $f_h$, which uses
   both the history of the target series and all the information from the complete training set $\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}$:
\[
\hat{S}_{t+h} = f_h\left(\{y_t^{(0)}, y_{t-1}^{(0)}, \dots, y_{t-s+1}^{(0)}\}; \mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}\right),
\]
where $s$ is a parameter that determines the length of the history used.

Two operators are defined over the set $\hat{S}_{t+h}$. The first operator,
 $\zeta(\cdot)$, checks whether the position $t+h$ is predictable based
 on the analysis of the distribution of values in $\hat{S}_{t+h}$:
\begin{equation}
\zeta(\hat{S}_{t+h}) =
\begin{cases}
1, & \text{if the position is predictable,} \\
0, & \text{otherwise.}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
The second operator, $g(\cdot)$, calculates a Unified Predicted Value
(UPV) for a predictable position:
\begin{equation}
\hat{y}_{t+h} = g(\hat{S}_{t+h}).
\end{equation}

Using these operators, the multi-step forecasting problem
 is formulated as a bi-criteria optimization. It is necessary to simultaneously
 minimize two functionals calculated on the test sample $Y_2^{(0)}$:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Functional of the number of non-predictable points ($I_1$):}
    \begin{equation}
    I_1 = \sum_{y_{t+h}^{(0)} \in Y_2^{(0)}} \left( 1 - \zeta(\hat{S}_{t+h}) \right) \to \min.
    \end{equation}

    \item \textbf{Functional of the average error on predictable points ($I_2$):}
    \begin{equation}
    I_2 = \frac{1}{|Y_{\text{pred}}^{(0)}|} \sum_{y_{t+h}^{(0)} \in Y_{\text{pred}}^{(0)}} \left| \hat{y}_{t+h} - y_{t+h}^{(0)} \right| \to \min,
    \end{equation}
\end{enumerate}
where $Y_{\text{pred}}^{(0)} \subseteq Y_2^{(0)}$ is the subset of points for
 which $\zeta(\hat{S}_{t+h}) = 1$. The present work aims to solve
  this bi-criteria problem by effectively using the extended
   training set $\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}$.

\subsection{Generation of Generalized z-Vectors and Sample Formation}

To effectively capture the non-local in-time correlations inherent in chaotic systems,
 we use the concept of generalized z-vectors, constructed based on predefined templates.

A template is an integer vector $\boldsymbol{k} = (k_1, k_2, \dots, k_{L-1})$,
 where $L$ is the length of the z-vector (the number of observations used), and $1 \le k_j \le K_{\max}$ is the lag between the $j$-th and $(j+1)$-th observation in the vector.

For each time series $Y^{(m)} \in \mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}$ and each template
 $\boldsymbol{k}$ from the set of all combinatorially possible templates $\aleph(L, K_{\max})$,
  its own sample of generalized z-vectors is generated. A z-vector at time $t$ is defined as:
\[
\mathbf{z}_{t}^{(m, \boldsymbol{k})} = \bigl(y^{(m)}_t,\ y^{(m)}_{t+k_1},\ y^{(m)}_{t+k_1+k_2},\ \dots,\ y^{(m)}_{t + \sum_{j=1}^{L-1} k_j}\bigr).
\]

The key step of our approach is the \textbf{aggregation} of all samples generated
 from the same template $\boldsymbol{k}$, but from different time series, into a single set:
\[
\mathcal{Z}^{(\boldsymbol{k})} = \bigcup_{m=0}^{M} \left\{ \mathbf{z}_{t}^{(m, \boldsymbol{k})} \right\}_{t}.
\]
Thus, for each template $\boldsymbol{k}$, a single, but significantly
 denser and more diverse training sample $\mathcal{Z}^{(\boldsymbol{k})}$ is formed,
  accumulating information about the dynamics of the entire family of systems.
\subsection{Clustering and Formation of the Motif Library}

Each combined sample $\mathcal{Z}^{(\boldsymbol{k})}$ is clustered separately
 to identify typical, repeating patterns of behavior. The centers of the resulting clusters
  are interpreted as \textit{motifs}.
A \textbf{motif}, corresponding to a template $\boldsymbol{k}$,
 is a vector $C_{\boldsymbol{k}} = (\eta_{\boldsymbol{k},1},
 \eta_{\boldsymbol{k},2}, \dots, \eta_{\boldsymbol{k},L})$, which is the
 center of a cluster in the space of z-vectors $\mathcal{Z}^{(\boldsymbol{k})}$.

The set of all motifs obtained for a template $\boldsymbol{k}$
is denoted by $\Psi_{\boldsymbol{k}}$. The complete \textit{motif library}
 $\Psi$ is the set of pairs (template, motifs) for all possible
  templates and is defined as follows:
\[
\Psi = \left\{ (\boldsymbol{k}, \Psi_{\boldsymbol{k}}) \mid \boldsymbol{k}
\in \aleph(L, K_{\max}) \right\}.
\]
This library encodes the generalized dynamics inherent to the entire class of systems
 under consideration and serves as the basis for constructing forecasts.

\subsection{Formation of the Set of Possible Predictions}

Forecasting the point $y_{t+h}^{(0)}$ of the target series $Y^{(0)}$
 is performed by searching the library $\Psi$ for motifs whose beginning
  corresponds to the recent history of the target series. The process consists of the following steps:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item For each template $\boldsymbol{k}$ from the history of the target
     series $\{y_{t}^{(0)}, y_{t-1}^{(0)}, \dots\}$, a \textit{query vector}
      $C_{\text{query}}^{(\boldsymbol{k})}$ is formed, consisting of $L-1$ elements
       and structurally identical to a truncated motif.

    \item This vector is compared with all truncated motifs $C_{\boldsymbol{k},
     \text{trunc}} = (\eta_{\boldsymbol{k},1}, \dots, \eta_{\boldsymbol{k}, L-1})$
     from the set $\Psi_{\boldsymbol{k}}$.

    \item If the Euclidean distance $\rho(C_{\text{query}}^{(\boldsymbol{k})},
     C_{\boldsymbol{k}, \text{trunc}})$ is less than a given threshold $\varepsilon$,
      then the last element of the corresponding full motif, $\eta_{\boldsymbol{k},L}$,
       is considered a possible prediction. The set of such predictions for a single template
       is denoted by $\hat{S}_{t+h}^{(\boldsymbol{k})}$.

    \item The final \textit{set of possible predictions} $\hat{S}_{t+h}$ for
     the point $y_{t+h}^{(0)}$ is the union of predictions obtained from all templates:
    \[
    \hat{S}_{t+h} = \bigcup_{\boldsymbol{k} \in \aleph(L, K_{\max})} \hat{S}_{t+h}^{(\boldsymbol{k})}.
    \]
\end{enumerate}

The resulting set $\hat{S}_{t+h}$ is an empirical distribution and serves as
 the basis for subsequent analysis. If the distribution of values in $\hat{S}_{t+h}$
  is dense and unimodal, the point is considered predictable, and a
  unique predictive value (UPV) is calculated for it. If the distribution is sparse,
   multimodal, or uniform, the point is classified as non-predictable.
    A detailed description of the classification methods and UPV calculation is presented in the next section.

\section{Forecast Calculation and Identification of Non-predictable Points}

The central stage of the proposed approach is the analysis of the empirical distribution
 $\hat{S}_{t+h}$, obtained for each forecasted point $y_{t+h}^{(0)}$. This
  analysis solves two interconnected tasks: determining whether the point is predictable,
   and, if so, calculating a unique predictive value (UPV) for it. In this work, both
   tasks are solved using a single tool---clustering the set $\hat{S}_{t+h}$ with the DBSCAN algorithm. \cite{Aggarwal2013}

\subsection{Integrated Approach Based on Clustering}

For each non-empty set $\hat{S}_{t+h}$, a clustering procedure is performed,
 which divides it into $N_c$ clusters $\{Q_1, Q_2, \dots, Q_{N_c}\}$. Let $|Q_j|$ be the number of
  elements (cardinality) of the $j$-th cluster. The clusters are ordered in descending order of cardinality:
   $|Q_1| \ge |Q_2| \ge \dots \ge |Q_{N_c}|$.

\textbf{Identification of non-predictable points} is based on the analysis of this
cluster structure. A point $y_{t+h}^{(0)}$ is considered \textit{predictable} if
 and only if there is a single dominant cluster in its set of predictions $\hat{S}_{t+h}$.
 Formally, this condition is expressed as follows:
\[
\zeta(\hat{S}_{t+h}) =
\begin{cases}
1, & \text{if } |Q_1| \ge \alpha \cdot |Q_2| \text{ (for } N_c > 1\text{) or } N_c = 1, \\
0, & \text{otherwise,}
\end{cases}
\]
where $\alpha > 1$ is a hyperparameter that sets the required degree of dominance of the
largest cluster. If the largest cluster is not at least $\alpha$ times larger than the
 second-largest cluster, it indicates the
 presence of several competing, equally probable future scenarios, which makes
  the forecast unreliable. In this case, the point is classified as \textit{non-predictable}.
   If the set $\hat{S}_{t+h}$ is empty, the point is also considered non-predictable.

\textbf{Calculation of the Unique Predictive Value (UPV)} is performed only for
 points classified as predictable ($\zeta(\hat{S}_{t+h})=1$). The final forecast $\hat{y}_{t+h}$ is taken as the arithmetic mean of the elements included in the largest (dominant) cluster $Q_1$:
\[
\hat{y}_{t+h} = \frac{1}{|Q_1|} \sum_{\hat{y} \in Q_1} \hat{y}.
\]
This approach allows filtering out noisy or less consistent groups of
predictions and basing the forecast on the most consensual and statistically significant scenario.

\subsection{"Daemon": The Ideal Classifier}

To assess the quality of the proposed method for identifying non-predictable points and to
determine the theoretical limits of forecast accuracy, a
control or ideal method is used in this work. This method assumes the availability of \textit{a priori}
 information about the true value $y_{t+h}^{(0)}$ at the analysis stage.

Within this approach, a point $y_{t+h}^{(0)}$ is considered \textit{non-predictable}
 if the error of the best possible prediction from the set $\hat{S}_{t+h}$ exceeds a given threshold $\delta$:
\[
\zeta_{\text{ideal}}(\hat{S}_{t+h}) =
\begin{cases}
0, & \text{if } \min_{\hat{y} \in \hat{S}_{t+h}} |\hat{y} - y_{t+h}^{(0)}| > \delta, \\
1, & \text{otherwise.}
\end{cases}
\]
In the experiments, the value $\delta = 0.05$ was used. This method,
 obviously, cannot be applied in practice, but it allows establishing a ground-truth
  labeling of non-predictable points and serves as a benchmark (upper quality bound)
   against which the results of the main, practical method are compared.
\subsection{Wishart's Clustering Algorithm}

To identify typical, repeating patterns (motifs) in each
combined sample of z-vectors $\mathcal{Z}^{(\boldsymbol{k})}$, a
 modified version of the Wishart clustering algorithm, proposed by Lapko
 and Chentsov \cite{GromovBaranov2023}, is applied. This method is based on a non-parametric estimation of
 data distribution density and concepts from graph theory, which makes it well
 suited for finding clusters of arbitrary shape in the phase space.

The algorithm is based on the concept of \textbf{significance} (or local density)
 of each point (z-vector) $x$ in the sample. The significance $p(x)$ is defined as
 the ratio of the number of nearest neighbors $r$ (a hyperparameter of the method) to the volume of the minimal
  hypersphere $V_r(x)$ containing these $r$ neighbors:
\begin{equation}
p(x) = \frac{r}{n \cdot V_r(x)},
\end{equation}
where $n$ is the total number of vectors in the sample. Intuitively, the smaller the volume $V_r(x)$,
the more densely the points are located around $x$, and the higher its significance.

In the second step, an \textbf{adjacency graph} $G$ is constructed, whose vertices are
 all the z-vectors of the sample. An edge between two vertices $x_i$ and $x_j$ is drawn if
  the distance between them $\rho(x_i, x_j)$ does not exceed the radius $d_r(x_i)$
  of the hypersphere constructed around the point $x_i$:
\[
(x_i, x_j) \in E \iff \rho(x_i, x_j) \le d_r(x_i),
\]
where $E$ is the set of edges of the graph.

The clustering process itself is iterative. All vectors in the sample are first
ordered in descending order of their significance $p(x)$. Then, they are sequentially
added to the model. For each new point $x_q$, three scenarios are possible:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item If $x_q$ is not connected by edges in the graph $G$ to any of the existing
    clusters, it forms a new, separate cluster.
    \item If $x_q$ is connected to the vertices of only one existing cluster,
     it joins that cluster.
    \item If $x_q$ acts as a bridge and connects the vertices of several previously
     independent clusters, these clusters are merged into one, and the point $x_q$ is also included in it.
\end{enumerate}

To determine the boundaries of clusters and prevent the merging of groups with significantly different
densities, a criterion of \textbf{significance by height} is used. A cluster
 $C_l$ is considered significant with respect to a threshold $\mu$ if the maximum density difference
  between any two points within it exceeds this threshold:
\begin{equation}
\max_{x_i, x_j \in C_l} |p(x_i) - p(x_j)| \ge \mu.
\end{equation}
This criterion allows distinguishing dense cluster cores from sparser
regions. If a candidate point attempts to merge several insignificant clusters,
 they are merged. If it is connected to a significant cluster, it is often classified
  as a noise or boundary point and is not included in any cluster. As a result
  of the algorithm's operation, a set of stable clusters is formed, the centers of which become the desired \textbf{motifs}.

\section{Study of the Lorenz System}

The Lorenz series, which has a fractal dimension of about 2, was chosen as the series for investigation.
 The main series \(Y\) was obtained with standard
  parameters: \(r = 28\), \(\sigma = 10\), \(b = 8/3\) using standard methods \cite{Balashova1991}.
   The behavior of the Lorenz system
   depends significantly on the value of the parameter \(r\), as described in detail in the
   works of \cite{KaplanYorke1979, Hateley2016}. The following characteristic regimes were found:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item For \(r < 1\): the system has a single stationary point (0,0,0).
    \item For \(1 < r < 13.926\): three stationary points appear.
    \item At \(r \approx 13.926\): homoclinic orbits arise.
    \item For \(13.926 < r < 24.06\): metastable chaos is observed, the chaotic
     set grows while remaining unstable and non-attracting.
    \item For \(24.06 < r < 24.74\): the attractor becomes attracting, while retaining two stable points.
    \item For \(r > 24.74\): a single global strange attractor is formed.
    \item For \(r > 50\): stable orbits appear.
    \item For \(144 < r < 167\): the system exhibits two periodic orbits.
    \item For \(r > 167\): intermittent chaos occurs with an alternation of chaotic and periodic regimes.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Visual Analysis and Data Characterization}

When working with chaotic systems, it is necessary to clearly distinguish between two
types of differences that arise when control parameters are changed.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Qualitative changes} that occur at bifurcation points.
     At these points, a fundamental restructuring of the system's dynamics takes place, and the shape (topology) of the attractor changes radically, for example, a chaotic attractor may be replaced by a stable periodic orbit.
    \item \textbf{Quantitative changes} observed between bifurcation
     points. In this case, the attractor retains its topological structure, but its metric properties change. The key change is the rate of exponential divergence of initially close trajectories: as a rule, the greater the difference in parameters (\(\Delta r\)), the faster the trajectories diverge.
\end{enumerate}

Figure \ref{fig:lorenz_comparison} shows segments of trajectories illustrating
precisely these quantitative differences. Since all the values of the parameter \(r\)
 chosen for the experiment are in the zone of stable chaos, the shape of the attractor remains unchanged.

A preliminary analysis of both visual (Figure~\ref{fig:lorenz_comparison})
 and quantitative (Table~\ref{tab:lorenz_metrics}) data would seem

   series. Visually, the trajectory for $r=28.0001$ almost coincides with the reference one

     confirmed by the quantitative metrics: this series demonstrates the minimum
      DTW distance and the maximum correlation, making it intuitively the most
      preferable candidate for extending the training sample.

However, anticipating the results that will be presented in detail in the experimental
 part, this conclusion, based on measures of global and visual similarity, turns out to be not
 just incomplete, but also misleading. It will be shown that it is precisely the series $r=28.0001$,
 despite its formal and visual proximity to the reference, that demonstrates weak predictive
 effectiveness. At the same time, the series at $r=27.99$, which both visually diverges earlier and is inferior
  in all metrics from the table, proves to be exceptionally useful in practice,
  in some cases even surpassing the quality of data augmentation from the target series itself.

This counter-intuitive result highlights the fundamental difference between global
 and local similarity of dynamics. Standard metrics, such as DTW or correlation,
 as well as simple visual comparison, evaluate the trajectories as a whole, averaging their behavior
 over the entire interval. However, the forecasting method used, based on the search for motifs,
  is \textit{local} by its nature. Its success depends not on how similar two
  trajectories are on average, but on whether the auxiliary series possesses a rich and relevant
  dictionary of short behavioral patterns that are also found in the target series.

Thus, the presented data are important not so much for directly choosing the best series,
but for demonstrating the inadequacy of standard metrics and superficial visual
analysis for this task. They convincingly show that the utility of an auxiliary series
is a non-trivial, emergent property, the assessment of which requires conducting a full
numerical experiment, as presented in the following sections.
\section{Experimental Research}

\subsection{Experiment 1: Influence of Training Sample Size and Composition}
\subsubsection{Experiment Description}
The main objective of this experiment was to quantitatively assess the influence of the size and
composition of the training sample on the quality of multi-step forecasting. The experimental
 setup was designed for a systematic investigation of the effect of data augmentation
 both from the target process itself and from external, but dynamically similar, systems.

The first coordinate of the Lorenz system, generated with canonical parameters ($\sigma = 10$, $r = 28$, $b = 8/3$), was used as the target time series $Y^{(0)}$.
 The initial training sample $Y_1^{(0)}$ consisted of 10,000 observations. For each of the investigated
  auxiliary series $Y^{(m)}$, generated with a parameter $r$ slightly different from
   the base one, the following iterative procedure was performed: segments of data from $Y^{(m)}$ were
   sequentially added to the initial sample $Y_1^{(0)}$ until the total size of the
   combined sample $\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}$ reached 40,000 points. The control
   case involved a similar increase in data volume, but using observations
    from the target series itself (i.e., $Y_1^{(0)}$ was expanded to 40,000 points without involving external data).

At each step of increasing the sample size, forecasting was performed for the resulting training
 set $\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}$ on a test set for a horizon of 10 steps ahead. To assess the forecast quality, three key metrics were calculated:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Proportion of Non-predictable points (NP):} Characterizes
    the model's confidence and its ability to find relevant motifs.
    \item \textbf{RMSE (Root Mean Squared Error):} Assesses the root mean squared error on predictable points.
    \item \textbf{MAPE (Mean Absolute Percentage Error):} Assesses
    the mean absolute percentage error on predictable points.
\end{itemize}
The identification of non-predictable points was performed using a practical
 method based on clustering the set $\hat{S}_{t+h}$ with the DBSCAN algorithm
 and verifying the dominance condition of the largest cluster.

\subsubsection{Analysis of Results}

    \caption{Dependence of metrics on the total training sample size for
    cases of effective extension (r = 28.1, 28.0001, -27.9, -27.99, 28).}
    \label{fig:main_exp}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Analysis of cases of metric improvement (Figure \ref{fig:main_exp}):} For auxiliary
 series with `r` parameters close to 28 (r=27.99, r=27.9), a significant (up to 6 times) reduction in the proportion
 of non-predictable points is observed, as well as a twofold reduction in RMSE. At the same time, the accuracy metrics RMSE
 and MAPE remain at a level comparable to the base case, or they improve. This confirms
 the hypothesis that adding data from close dynamical systems increases the robustness
  of the model without a significant loss of accuracy. However, for the value r = 28.0001,
  the number of non-predictable points decreases logarithmically, but the quality
   metrics worsen in most measurements.

The obtained results (Figure \ref{fig:main_exp}) convincingly prove
 that data augmentation from systems with close parameters (e.g., \(r=27.99\))
  can significantly improve forecast quality. However, it was also found
   that not every formally close value of the parameter \(r\) yields a positive effect.
   To systematically investigate this phenomenon and determine the boundaries of the utility of external
   data, the following experiment was conducted.

\subsection{Experiment 3: Systematic Investigation of the Parameter r}
\subsubsection{Experiment Description}

The experimental setup was defined as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Target series} \(Y^{(0)}\) was generated by the Lorenz system with the
     canonical parameter \(r_0 = 28\).
    
    \item \textbf{Base training sample} \(\mathcal{D}_{\text{base}}\) was
     formed from the first \(N_{\text{base}} = 5000\) observations of the target series:
    \[
    \mathcal{D}_{\text{base}} = \{y_0^{(0)}, y_1^{(0)}, \dots, y_{N_{\text{base}}-1}^{(0)}\}.
    \]
    
    \item \textbf{Auxiliary series.} A set of auxiliary
     time series \(\{Y^{(r)}\}\) was generated for each value of the parameter \(r\) from the investigated set \(\mathcal{R}\).
    
    \item \textbf{Hybrid training samples.} For each \(r \in \mathcal{R}\),
     a hybrid training sample \(\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}^{(r)}\) was formed
     by combining the base sample \(\mathcal{D}_{\text{base}}\) with a segment of
     the first \(N_{\text{aux}} = 5000\) observations of the corresponding auxiliary series \(Y^{(r)}\):
    \[
    \mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}^{(r)} = \mathcal{D}_{\text{base}} \cup \{y_0^{(r)},
     y_1^{(r)}, \dots, y_{N_{\text{aux}}-1}^{(r)}\}.
    \]
    Thus, for all \(r\), the size of the hybrid sample remained constant: \(|\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}^{(r)}| = N_{\text{base}} + N_{\text{aux}} = 10000\).
    
    \item \textbf{Quality Assessment.} For each sample \(\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}^{(r)}\),
     forecasting was performed on the test data set (from series \(Y^{(0)}\)), and
      the values of the quality functionals were calculated: \(RMSE(r)\) and \(NP(r)\).
\end{itemize}

The results, presented as functions of the parameter \(r\), were compared with two control levels:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Forecast quality on a small sample, \(\text{RMSE}(\mathcal{D}_{\text{base}})\) and
     \(\text{NP}(\mathcal{D}_{\text{base}})\).
    \item Forecast quality on a "pure" sample of equivalent size, consisting
     only of data from the target series, \(\mathcal{D}_{\text{target\_large}} = \{y_0^{(0)}, \dots, y_{9999}^{(0)}\}\).
\end{enumerate}
\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{../deviations/Разные_r.png}
    \caption{Comparison of metrics when adding 5000 points from a series with a different
    parameter r to 5000 points of the main series.}
    \label{fig:different_r}
\end{figure}
\subsubsection{Analysis of the Experiment}

The results (Figure \ref{fig:different_r}) show that there is a narrow vicinity (26, 30)
 around `r=28` in which the addition of auxiliary series leads to
  an improvement in metrics compared to the base sample of 5000 points. This vicinity
  is in no way related to the bifurcation diagram.
  In the best cases, a 1.5-fold reduction in RMSE and NP is achieved.
   However, even in the best cases, the forecast quality is inferior to the results
    obtained on a sample of 10,000 points from the original series.

To explain the observed effects, a hypothesis was proposed that
 the addition of relevant external data does not "blur" the original structure
 of motifs but, on the contrary, stabilizes and enriches it. To test this
 hypothesis, an analysis of the displacement of cluster centroids was conducted.

\subsection{Experiment 4: Analysis of Centroid Displacement}

\subsubsection{Experiment Description}

A motif library $\Psi_{\text{base}}$, built on the training sample $Y_1^{(0)}$ consisting of 10,000 observations of the target
series (Lorenz system, $r=28$), was used as the reference model. Then, similarly to Experiment 1, an iterative
procedure was performed: data segments from the auxiliary series $Y^{(m)}$ were sequentially added to the initial sample $Y_1^{(0)}$
 until the total size of the combined sample $\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}$
 reached 40,000 points. At each step of increasing the data volume, a new, \textit{hybrid motif library} $\Psi_{\text{mixed}}$ was built for the resulting set
  $\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}$.

To assess the structural divergence at each step, the average
 minimum distance metric $D_{\text{avg}}$ between the reference and hybrid libraries was calculated.
 For each motif $C_{\text{base}} \in \Psi_{\text{base}}$, a search was performed for the closest
 motif in the hybrid library $\Psi_{\text{mixed}}$, after which the average
 value of these minimum Euclidean distances was calculated over all motifs from the reference library:
\[
D_{\text{avg}} = \frac{1}{|\Psi_{\text{base}}|} \sum_{C_{\text{base}} \in \Psi_{\text{base}}}
 \left( \min_{C_{\text{mixed}} \in \Psi_{\text{mixed}}} \rho(C_{\text{base}}, C_{\text{mixed}}) \right),
\]
where $\rho(\cdot, \cdot)$ is the Euclidean distance.
\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{../centroids_diff/plot_avg_distance.png}
    \caption{Average Euclidean distance between motifs of the main series and the mixed sample.}
    \label{fig:avg_distance}
\end{figure}
\subsubsection{Analysis of the Experiment}
It is observed (Figure \ref{fig:avg_distance}) that when a
significant volume of data from the auxiliary series is added, the average
 distance between the centroids begins to decrease logarithmically.
  This may indicate that the added data stabilize
   the cluster structure, making the centroids more robust.



\subsection{Discussion and Validation of Results}

\subsubsection{Experiment 1.1: Validation using an ideal classifier}

\subparagraph{Experiment Description}
The objective of this experiment was to verify and validate the results obtained in Experiment 1, and to establish the theoretical upper bound for the efficiency of the proposed sample extension method. The experimental setup was identical to that described in Experiment 1: the same target and auxiliary series were used, along with the same metrics (RMSE, MAPE, NP) and the same method for calculating UPV as the mean of the largest cluster.

The sole and key difference was the method of identifying unpredictable points. Instead of the practical method based on cluster structure analysis (dominance of a single cluster), an ideal classifier, described in the "Daemon" section, was used. A point was considered unpredictable if the error of even the best possible prediction from the set $\hat{S}_{t+h}$ exceeded the threshold $\delta = 0.05$.

    \caption{Dependence of metrics on the total training sample size for a priori classification}
    \label{fig:daempon_apr}
\end{figure}
\subparagraph{Analysis of Results}
The results of this experiment (Figure \ref{fig:daempon_apr}) fully confirmed the conclusions drawn in Experiment 1, demonstrating the same dependence of forecast quality on the choice of the auxiliary series. As in the previous experiment, the series with parameters $r=27.99$ and $r=27.9$ proved to be effective data donors, leading to a significant reduction in the proportion of unpredictable points while maintaining high accuracy. Similarly, the series with $r=28.01$ and more distant parameters again showed a degradation in forecast quality.

The key difference in the results is that the ideal classifier, by definition, filtered out all points where the forecast was known to be inaccurate. This allowed for achieving a nearly constant and minimal error (RMSE, MAPE) across the entire forecasting horizon for the remaining predictable points.

\subparagraph{Experiment Description}
This experiment tested the hypothesis that the target series could be predicted using a training sample consisting exclusively of data from auxiliary series. The experiment was completely analogous to Experiment 1, except that the base series was not included in the training sample.

\section{Conclusion}

This work has successfully addressed a fundamental problem in forecasting chaotic systems—the scarcity of data for reliable attractor reconstruction. The hypothesis that this deficit can be overcome by incorporating data from dynamically similar but formally independent systems was proposed and experimentally validated.

The results demonstrate that the proposed method of inter-series data extension is a powerful tool. The key advantage of the approach is that it allows for a dramatic improvement in forecast quality without requiring additional observations from the target series itself. In the best cases, without increasing the size of the target sample, it was possible to reduce the proportion of unpredictable points by a factor of six and to halve the root mean square error. This indicates that a model trained on a hybrid dataset acquires the ability to find relevant behavioral patterns for a much wider range of states.

However, the key and most non-trivial result of the work was the discovery of the complex nature of similarity in dynamical systems. A positive effect from augmentation is not guaranteed and depends on the nonlinear compatibility of the dynamics, rather than on the formal proximity of control parameters. For instance, data from a system with the parameter \(r=27.99\) significantly improved the forecast, while data from a system with an even closer parameter, \(r=28.01\), did not lead to an improvement in metrics.

This phenomenon demonstrates the inadequacy of standard global similarity metrics (DTW, Pearson correlation) for this task. Success is determined not by the overall similarity of trajectories, but by the compatibility of their attractors, reconstructed using motifs. The analysis of centroid displacement (Experiment 4) showed that relevant external data do not dilute but, on the contrary, stabilize the library of motifs, making it more complete and robust.

Thus, this study resolves the initial problem of data scarcity but poses a new, more complex challenge: the development of formal criteria for the a priori assessment of the prognostic compatibility of time series. The creation of such a mathematical framework would allow a shift from empirical data selection to an algorithmic one, opening up possibilities for applying the method to the analysis of real, noisy data in fields such as financial markets, climatology, biomedical signals, or the study of energy consumption patterns \cite{PerezChacon2018}, where obtaining long stationary samples is often impossible.
